The Tariff Problems Nobody Talks About

Tariff mistakes that are silently eating your Amazon margins right now

US-China trade policy changes faster than most sellers can track — and the cost of being wrong lands directly on your profit and loss, not your customs broker's.

  • 🎯

    Your HTS code is wrong — and you've been overpaying for years without knowing

    HTS classification is genuinely complex. Similar products can fall under codes with dramatically different duty rates. We regularly find sellers using codes that attract 25% tariffs when correct classification would attract 7.5% or less. On $200,000 of annual imports, that difference is $35,000 — appearing nowhere in your logistics invoice, only in your landed cost.

  • 📋

    Your customs documentation is incomplete — and you won't know until there's a problem

    Missing or incorrect documentation doesn't cause delays every time — but when it does, the timing is always catastrophic. Goods held at port during Q4, a customs audit you're not prepared for, or an entry rejection that delays stock by three weeks. The documentation your broker sent looked fine. It wasn't.

  • 🛤️

    You're routing shipments through ports that maximise your duty exposure

    Port of entry, shipping route, and the specific structure of how your goods are consolidated can all affect the effective tariff rate you pay. Most sellers ship the same way they always have — out of habit, not optimisation. The difference between a smart routing strategy and a legacy one can be substantial on higher-volume shipments.

    CBP (US Customs and Border Protection) audits are increasing for eCommerce importers. If you can't produce complete, accurate records for every shipment — HTS codes, entered values, origin documentation, and duty payments — the penalties can exceed what the original tariff savings would have been. Most sellers couldn't produce these records without significant scrambling.

    You're selling in the UK and EU but navigating VAT, import duties, and customs separately for each market

FAQ

Questions about tariff management

What is an HTS code and why does it matter so much?
HTS (Harmonized Tariff Schedule) codes are the classification system US Customs uses to determine the duty rate applied to imported goods. The same physical product can sometimes be classified under codes with very different duty rates depending on its specific construction, materials, or end use. Using an incorrect code — even unintentionally — can result in overpayment, underpayment (which creates audit liability), or shipment delays during customs review.
What are Section 301 tariffs and do they apply to my products?
Section 301 tariffs are additional duties imposed by the US on goods imported from China, initially introduced in 2018 as part of trade disputes between the two countries. They apply to a very wide range of product categories and are levied on top of standard import duties. Rates range from 7.5% to 25% depending on the specific HTS code. Contact us and we'll confirm whether your products are affected and at what rate.
What is a CBP Form 7501 and do I need it?
Form 7501 (Customs Entry Summary) is the official record of a US import entry — including the HTS classification used, declared value, and duties paid. As the importer of record, you're required to maintain these records for five years. They're also essential for your accountant to accurately calculate cost of goods and for any CBP audit. We provide an extract of this for every consolidated shipment we process for you.
Can you help if I'm already under a CBP audit or review?
If you're facing a CBP inquiry or audit, the first priority is gathering complete documentation for the shipments in question. We can provide all records for shipments processed through our facility. For active audit situations, we recommend also engaging a licensed customs broker or trade attorney in addition to our support.
Do you manage tariffs for markets outside the US?
Yes. We manage customs compliance for all markets we ship to — including UK (HMRC import procedures and UK Global Tariff), EU (Import One-Stop Shop and EU Customs Union), Australia (DIBP), Canada (CBSA), Japan, and others. Each market has its own requirements and we handle the documentation and compliance for each.
